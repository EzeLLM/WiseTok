Wandering Through Kyoto: A First-Time Visitor's Love Letter

Standing in the wooden machiya house's open corridor, I finally understand why people speak of Kyoto with such reverence. It's not just the temples, though there are those too—layer upon layer of them, some built eight centuries ago, others restored last year. It's the spaces between. The way autumn light filters through the grating of a wooden shopfront on a quiet side street. The sound of a bell at closing time, called osusume by the shopkeeper, a gentle reminder that tradition hasn't been automated away.

I arrived on a Tuesday evening with the kind of jet lag that makes time feel like taffy. My ryokan—a traditional inn with tatami mats and a shared onsen—was tucked into a narrow alley in the Higashiyama district. The owner, Yuki-san, greeted me with such genuine warmth that I nearly wept from gratigue and relief. She showed me how to properly enter the onsen (shoes off before the wooden gate), how to wash thoroughly before entering the communal bath, and most importantly, she recommended a small restaurant she had known for thirty years: "Kore wa sekai no naka de saikou desu." This is the best in the world.

The first full day, I walked without a map. This was partly hubris and partly strategy. I figured that Kyoto, being designed as a grid, would forgive wandering. I was right. I stumbled upon the Fushimi Inari shrine around 9 AM, before the tour groups arrived, and found myself alone walking beneath thousands of vermillion torii gates stacked like a tunnel toward the mountain. My legs ached. The sun was intense. And yet I felt unaccountably happy, the kind of joy that seems physiologically impossible to manufacture—it has to be stumbled upon.

I sat on a stone bench and made notes in my journal:
- Woman in traditional dress carrying flower arrangement
- Three monks in saffron robes photographing each other
- Cicada sound, precise and relentless
- Smell of incense curling from a side shrine
- My own breathing

By the third day, I had developed a routine. Morning matcha at a café in Arashiyama run by a woman who spoke no English but whose espresso drink technique was flawless. Afternoon temple-walking—I visited Ryoan-ji, Kinkaku-ji, and Kiyomizu-dera—each famous, each crushing in its own way. Evenings, I would wander the backstreets and eat at tiny restaurants where the menu was only in Japanese and the chef assumed you would like whatever he recommended. At a soba shop, I learned the word "gochisousama"—literally "it was a treat," said at the end of a meal to thank the chef and acknowledge that the meal was a gift, not merely a transaction.

The Bamboo Grove near Arashiyama deserves special mention, though not for reasons the guidebooks outline. Yes, the density of bamboo is overwhelming; the stalks are pale green and enormous. But what struck me was the silence. In a country famous for organized mass tourism, someone had created a path where every visitor must be quiet—not by rule, but by the overwhelming presence of so much living green. I heard my own footsteps, the rustle of leaves, a bird whose name I don't know. A small group of tourists ahead of me fell silent unprompted.

By the second week, something had shifted. I stopped thinking of myself as a tourist and started thinking of myself as a temporary resident. I knew which convenience store sold the best inari-zushi. I had developed an understanding with the barista at my morning matcha place—she would pour before I asked. I could navigate the train system without checking my phone. I moved through the city with the kind of unconscious confidence that comes only with repetition.

On my last night, Yuki-san invited me to dinner at her home instead of recommending a restaurant. We sat cross-legged at a low table and she served miso soup, grilled fish, pickled vegetables, and rice that tasted nothing like rice I had eaten before. We had almost no shared language. She showed me photographs of her children, now grown and living in Tokyo. I showed her pictures of my family in California. We made gestures and smiled. At the end, she pressed a small cloth wrapping into my hands—a bookmark she had embroidered, with my name in Japanese hiragana: えれんor—a phonetic rendering of my name in the Japanese syllabary.

Flying out the next morning, I was already nostalgic. Kyoto had not changed me in any dramatic way. I was returning to the same life, the same work, the same anxieties. But something about the way the autumn light fell, the quiet of the bamboo grove, the kindness of Yuki-san, the taste of water-clear tea at a thousand-year-old temple—these things had lodged themselves somewhere permanent. The Japanese have a word, mono no aware—the pathos of things, the beauty in transience and incompleteness. I finally understood it not as a concept but as a feeling, and that feeling is what I carry home.
