The Paradox of Moral Knowledge: On the Epistemology of Ethical Judgment

Abstract: This essay argues that the traditional binary distinction between moral realism and anti-realism obscures a more fundamental problem—namely, that moral claims can simultaneously possess the characteristics of both objective knowledge and subjective construction, depending on the epistemic framework within which we examine them. Drawing on work by Kant, Hume, and contemporary analytic ethics, I contend that moral epistemology requires a framework that accommodates genuine normativity without collapsing into either relativism or a problematic form of metaphysical commitment.

I. The Problem Stated

Consider the following moral propositions: "Slavery is wrong." "We have obligations to our children." "Honesty is virtuous." Most of us believe these statements express truths about the world, not merely opinions or preferences. We hold them with conviction, we teach them to our children, we base policies upon them. Yet the epistemological status of these truths remains puzzling. What, precisely, is the structure of moral knowledge? How do we come to know these truths? And perhaps more troublingly, in what sense can they be true?

The realist answer seems straightforward: moral facts exist independently of our beliefs about them, much as physical facts do. We discover moral truths through some faculty of moral perception or intuition, perhaps refined through rational reflection and dialogue. This position carries intuitive appeal. When we condemn slavery, we feel we are responding to something real—a genuine wrongness in the institution, not a mere projection of our subjective preferences onto a morally neutral world.

Yet the realist position faces a formidable challenge, articulated most clearly in modern form by J.L. Mackie's "argument from queerness." If moral facts exist, Mackie asked, what is their metaphysical nature? How do they causally interact with the physical world? They cannot be physical properties, for no amount of empirical investigation into the structure of slavery—its economic arrangements, its psychological effects—will ever yield the property of wrongness. Physical science is silent on normativity. Yet if moral properties are non-physical, how do we access knowledge of them? What faculty detects them? The realist is forced to posit either some mysterious non-natural property or an equally mysterious faculty of moral intuition. Ipso facto, realism becomes implausible.

The anti-realist response abandons the claim that moral facts exist independently. Moral judgments, on this view, are expressions of attitude, preference, or commitment—useful for coordinating behavior, perhaps, but not the sort of thing that can be true or false in any robust sense. When I say "slavery is wrong," I am really expressing disapproval, or committing to a principle, or reporting the feelings shared by my society. This position avoids the metaphysical difficulties that plague realism. Yet it seems to undermine the force and objectivity of our moral convictions. If moral claims are merely expressions of feeling, then the Nazi who felt no moral objection to genocide stands on equal epistemic ground as the abolitionist who condemned slavery. This is intuitively unacceptable.

II. The Kantian Synthesis

One might argue, following Kant, that this dilemma arises from a false dichotomy. Kant distinguished sharply between objects as they appear to us in experience and objects as they are in themselves. Our moral sense is not a faculty for detecting pre-existing moral facts in a mind-independent world. Rather, it is the structure of our own rational agency. When I judge an action to be wrong, I am not discovering a mysterious moral property. I am determining whether the action is compatible with a principle I could rationally will as a law for all rational agents.

This Kantian approach offers significant advantages. It explains why moral claims carry a sense of objectivity without requiring metaphysically dubious moral entities. Morality emerges from the nature of rational agency itself. The wrongness of slavery is not a free-floating property in the universe; it is a feature of what rational agents—agents like ourselves—could coherently endorse. This grounds moral knowledge in reason rather than in intuition or in the contingencies of human feeling.

Yet the Kantian approach faces a persistent objection: in what sense can my own rational commitments constitute knowledge of moral truth? One might argue that Kant has merely relocated the problem. Rather than explaining how we know about independent moral facts, he has explained how we commit ourselves to moral principles. These are not the same thing. If morality is merely the expression of my rational nature, then moral claims remain, in some sense, subjective—they tell us about the structure of our agency, not about the world.

III. A Proposed Resolution

The path forward, one might argue, lies in recognizing that the very distinction between "objective knowledge of the world" and "subjective construction" may not cleanly apply to moral knowledge. Here, I draw a parallel to our knowledge of mathematics. No one supposes that numbers exist as physical objects in spacetime. Yet we confidently speak of mathematical truths and of the knowledge we possess about them. Mathematical truths are mind-dependent in the sense that they emerge from and supervene upon the rational practices of mathematical communities. Yet they are objective in that they hold regardless of individual preference or belief.

Moral knowledge may function similarly. Moral truths supervene upon facts about rational agency, human flourishing, and social coordination. They are not written into the fabric of the universe as independent facts. Yet they are not arbitrary either. A proposition such as "unnecessary suffering is bad" is true because of facts about the nature of suffering, the valence of human experience, and the commitments of rational agents to themselves and each other. This truth cannot be wished away by individual preference, yet neither does it require mysterious non-natural properties.

One might object: but mathematical truths, unlike moral truths, do not depend on human interests. Two plus two equals four regardless of whether anyone cares. Does this not show that mathematical knowledge is objective in a way moral knowledge cannot be?

The objection carries less force than it initially appears. It is true that mathematical truths are indifferent to human interests. But this is precisely because mathematics is an abstract formal system, abstracted away from the concerns of agents. Morality, by contrast, is constitutively about agency, about how agents should act and relate to one another. It would be conceptually confused to expect moral truths to be indifferent to facts about agency—just as it would be confused to expect the rules of chess to be indifferent to the practices of chess players.

IV. Implications and Limitations

This proposal suggests that moral knowledge is neither purely objective (in the sense of realism) nor purely subjective (in the sense of anti-realism). Rather, it is intersubjective. Moral truths hold because of facts about how rational agents must, in principle, relate to one another. A society that practices slavery violates something genuinely true—not because slavery has the non-natural property of wrongness, but because slavery violates the principles that rational agents committed to mutual recognition and dignity must endorse.

This framework vindicates the intuition that moral claims are robustly true, while avoiding the metaphysical implausibilities of realism. It explains why moral disagreement is possible and why moral progress is intelligible. Yet it also resists the relativism that anti-realism seems to invite.

Nevertheless, this approach leaves significant questions unresolved. If moral truths supervene on facts about rational agency, what exactly are the relevant facts? How do we determine which principles rational agents "must" endorse? These are not trivial questions, and reasonable people may disagree profoundly about the answers. A society might claim that patriarchy supervenes on rational agency; another might deny this claim. How do we adjudicate such disputes?

Here, one must acknowledge the limits of epistemological theory. Philosophy cannot short-circuit the hard work of moral reasoning, dialogue, and deliberation. What it can do—what this essay has attempted—is clarify the structure of our moral knowledge-claims, showing that we need not choose between Scylla's realism and Charybdis' anti-realism. Moral knowledge is real, mind-dependent, and grounded in the rational agency of ourselves and others. This is perhaps less metaphysically satisfying than either alternative, yet it may be the most honest description of what moral knowledge actually is.

V. Conclusion

The paradox with which we began—that moral claims seem both objective and subjective—dissolves once we recognize that the dichotomy itself is misleading. Moral truths are best understood as truths about how rational agents, understood as free and equal, must relate to one another. This grounds morality in something real (the structure of rational agency and human interdependence) without requiring metaphysical exotica (non-natural moral properties or faculties that detect them). Whether this resolves the problem to the satisfaction of realist and anti-realist alike remains to be seen. But it offers a way forward—a middle path that preserves what is true and valuable in both traditions while avoiding their respective pitfalls.
